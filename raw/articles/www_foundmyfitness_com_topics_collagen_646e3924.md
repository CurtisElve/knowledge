A study of 274 people between the ages of 18 and 80 years with active rheumatoid arthritis found
that hydrolyzed collagen reduced pain, stiffness, and disease activity.
The study authors randomized participants to receive a placebo or one of four doses (20, 100, 500,
or 2,500 micrograms) of hydrolyzed type II chicken collagen in a diluted liquid of acetic acid (an
organic compound) daily for 24 weeks.
The clinical assessments of pain, stiffness, and disease activity included three criteria: the
Paulus criteria, the American College of Rheumatology criteria, and a reduction of greater than 30
percent in both tender and swollen joint counts.
The participants were required to discontinue treatment with disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs.

More studies are needed to determine the extent to which hydrolyzed or undenatured collagen can
improve symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis and the appropriate dose that may elicit benefits.

Collagen is vital for maintaining the structural integrity of every tissue in the body.
Aging decreases the body's capacity to synthesize collagen, contributing to skin aging, joint
disease, and diminished bone health.
Oral hydrolyzed collagen supplementation represents an inexpensive and convenient strategy to offset
some of the physiological declines associated with aging.
In particular, hydrolyzed collagen supplementation may improve various aspects of skin aging,
decrease pain associated with osteoporosis, increase bone mineral density in postmenopausal women,
and improve metabolic health in people with type 2 diabetes and hypertension.
More studies are needed to distinguish the therapeutic benefits between the types of collagen (types
I, II, and III) and the source (marine, bovine, chicken, and pig).
